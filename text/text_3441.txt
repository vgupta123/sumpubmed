adam  is a family of membrane-spanning multi-domain proteins containing a metalloproteinase-like domain and a disintegrin-like domain. currently, more than  <dig> adams have been identified in mammals. their biological activities implicate adams in fertilization, myogenesis and neurogenesis by proteolysis and adhesion. some types of adam are catalytically active metalloproteases and shed the extracellular domains of membrane-bound growth factors or receptors  <cit> . for example, adam <dig>  has been shown to cleave several substrates, including tumour necrosis factor alpha <cit> , heparin-binding epidermal growth factor-like growth factor  <cit>  and transforming growth factor alpha  <cit> . studies of adam17-null mice have revealed that adam <dig> is critical in embryogenesis and plays an essential role in the supply of growth factors  <cit> . adams are also involved in cell-cell or cell-matrix adhesion through their interaction with integrins or syndecans. more than  <dig> adams have been shown to support integrin-mediated cell adhesion in vitro  <cit> . it has been reported that adam2-null and adam3-null male mutants are infertile and their spermatozoa lack egg-binding abilities  <cit> . both adam <dig> and adam <dig> are not metalloproteases because they lack catalytic site sequences in their metalloprotease domain. these studies clearly showed that non-proteinase members of adams also have significant roles in vivo.

we have reported the findings of adam <dig>  adam <dig> and adam <dig> genes and their restricted expression in the human and murine nervous systems  <cit> . sequence analysis suggests that they are not metalloproteases, since they all lack a catalytic motif. it has been reported that adam <dig> protein is localised to the cell surface  <cit> , interacts with alpha-v beta- <dig> integrin heterodimer  <cit>  and the disruption of adam <dig> gene in the mouse results in premature death associated with ataxia and tremor  <cit> . although the cause of death in this mouse is unknown, for these phenotypes, impaired cell-cell or cell-matrix interactions in the nervous system caused by loss of adam <dig> may be responsible. adam <dig> and adam <dig> share highly homologous sequences in their extracellular domains. especially, it is evident in their putative integrin binding loop sequences, cravncd, which is located centre of the disintegrin domain. these findings led us to hypothesize that adam <dig> is an integrin binder and plays an important role in the nervous system, as does adam <dig>  to determine the physiological functions of adam <dig>  we generated and analysed adam <dig> gene-targeted mice.

discussion
in the present study, we examined the function of adam <dig> by generating mice that lacked the adam <dig> gene. adam22-deficient mice exhibited severe ataxia and premature death. in contrast, heterozygous mutants were healthy, fertile and survived more than  <dig> year without obvious abnormalities. the cause of death in the homozygous mutants is not clear; however, it is likely to be due to multiple seizures. this is because convulsive seizures were occasionally observed in homozygotes, after which the suffered mice appeared exhausted and, in most cases, died on the next day. it is known that cortical dysplasia resulting from aberrant brain development causes seizure syndrome  <cit> . for example, mice lacking the neuron-specific activator of cyclin  <dig>  p <dig>  exhibit seizures and a severe neuronal migration defect  <cit> . because adam <dig> protein is expressed on the cell surface and is likely to be involved in cell-cell or cell-matrix interactions, we hypothesized that depletion of adam <dig> would generate aberrant neuronal migration. histochemical analysis was performed to verify this hypothesis. however, no marked abnormalities were observed in the mutant mouse brain, ruling out a critical role for adam <dig> in neuronal migration. another possible explanation is that the premature death in first  <dig> weeks of life is caused by the dysfunction of the autonomic nervous system. especially, nerves which control the breathing would be very important, because the respiration system undergoes significant maturation in the first 2â€“ <dig> weeks after birth. for example, the myelin-deficient  rat, which carries a point mutation in the proteolipid protein  gene, exhibits ataxia, tremor, dysmyelination and dies at approximately postnatal day  <dig>  in md rat, early death is caused by the dysfunction of the brain stem which is essential for autonomic control of respiration during hypoxia  <cit> . further study is needed to verify the cause of death in adam22-deficient mice.

another interesting phenotype observed in adam22-deficient mice was hypomyelinogenesis. histopathological analysis revealed marked hypomyelination in the sciatic and trigeminal nerves. however, myelin formation in the spinal cord and the brain was normal. it was therefore evident that hypomyelinogenesis occurred specifically in the peripheral nerves in the knockout mice, suggesting schwann cell dysfunction. in the mutant sciatic nerve, the density of pro-myelinating schwann cells  was markedly higher than that seen in heterozygotes. higher pro-myelinating schwann cell density and marked delay in myelin formation indicate that adam <dig> plays an important role in schwann cell differentiation, but not in proliferation.

investigations on why hypomyelinogenesis occurred in adam22-deficient mice centre on integrin. this is because several adams have been shown to interact with integrins via disintegrin domains  <cit> , and it has been suggested that alpha- <dig> beta- <dig> integrin on the schwann cell plays an important role in myelin formation  <cit> . in addition, laminin- <dig> is a key player in peripheral myelin formation, because laminin- <dig> deficiency causes dysmyelination in mice and humans, and its receptors, alpha- <dig> beta- <dig> integrin and dystroglycan, are also shown to be deeply involved in myelin formation  <cit> . the conditional knockout studies of beta- <dig> integrin and laminin gamma- <dig> in schwann cell suggested that both laminin- <dig> and integrins are essential for segregation of large bundles of axons in the early stage of myelin formation  <cit> . in contrast, the phenotype seen in the adam22-deficient mice was quite different: for example, unsorted bundles of axons were not seen in nerves and roots. we assume that adam <dig> partially modulates integrin-mediated signals or is involved at a later stage of myelin formation.
