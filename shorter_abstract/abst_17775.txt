recent studies in invertebrates have reported transgenerational plasticity in phenotypic responses of offspring when the mothers had been previously exposed to either live or heat-killed pathogens.
the direction of the transgenerational effects was not consistent with that predicted under a scenario of adaptive transgenerational plasticity.
prospective mothers were assigned to a non-infectious pathogen treatment consisting of an injection with heat-killed bacteria or a procedural control, and a stress treatment consisting of sleep deprivation or control.
these interactions were unaffected by sleep deprivation.
instead, they were indicative of expectations based on terminal investment.
here, we explored the transgenerational consequences of a dual pathogen and stress challenge administered in the maternal generation in the fruit fly, drosophila melanogaster.
we observed transgenerational interactions involving pathogen treatments of mothers and their offspring, on the reproductive success of daughters but not sons.
understanding whether this plasticity is adaptive requires a factorial design in which both mothers and their offspring are subjected to either the pathogen challenge or a control, in experimentally matched and mismatched combinations.
most prior studies exploring the capacity for pathogen-mediated transgenerational plasticity have, however, failed to adopt such a design.
